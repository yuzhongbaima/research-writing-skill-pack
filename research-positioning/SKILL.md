# Research Positioning

Use this skill after some literature understanding already exists.

Default stance: a good engineering paper is positioned by practical significance plus defensible evidence, not by novelty theater alone.

## Main job

Compress broad notes into a narrower, arguable positioning statement.

## Inputs

Useful inputs include:

- review notes
- candidate research questions
- pain points or observed limitations in the literature
- target venue or paper type if known

## Workflow

1. Restate the paper in plain language.
2. Identify the specific decision or knowledge problem.
3. State why the problem matters in engineering terms:
   - safety
   - reliability
   - energy or emissions
   - cost or efficiency
   - robustness
   - deployability
3. Separate:
   - evidence-backed field pattern
   - inferred gap
   - proposed paper response
4. Draft 2 to 4 thesis variants.
5. Test each variant against:
   - novelty
   - defensibility
   - scope control
   - fit with available evidence
   - engineering significance
6. Produce a preferred positioning with 2 to 3 contribution bullets.

## Good outputs

- one-paragraph problem framing
- one-sentence gap statement
- thesis options with tradeoffs
- tentative contributions that do not overclaim

## Guardrails

- Avoid claiming a gap just because a niche combination of keywords is rare.
- Avoid importing machine-learning novelty norms into engineering papers when the real contribution is validation depth, system integration, or operational insight.
- Avoid framing a tooling workflow as a research contribution unless the paper's real contribution is methodological or empirical.
- If the literature only supports a modest claim, keep the thesis modest.
